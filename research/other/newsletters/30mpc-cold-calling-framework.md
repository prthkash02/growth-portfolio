## Why cold calling fails

Most cold calls fail on predictable tactics: canned telemarketer openers, buzzword pitches ("single source of truth"), and aggressive objection handling that creates resistance instead of dialogue. The goal is to make the prospect **forget it's a cold call** — reveal personality, show industry expertise, and demonstrate genuine listening so the call shifts from rejection to conversation.

## Step 1 — Context-first openers

Lead with prospect-specific context rather than "How's your day?" / "Bad time?".

- **"Heard the name tossed around" opener** — establish peer credibility in seconds: lead with familiar context (competitors they know, regional/investor ties), introduce yourself as an established player, ask if they've heard of you. Even a "no" positions you inside their world, not as a cold caller.
- **Tailored permission opener** — own the cold call: give context tied to a specific business trigger, acknowledge the interruption with humor, then ask permission to pitch with a time commitment. Perceived control increases genuine listening.

## Step 2 — The Problem Proposition

Generic value props fail. Use three elements:
1. **Triggering problem** — paint a vivid, specific scene (who/where/what + emotional context).
2. **One-sentence solution** — your core differentiator as the antidote to that pain.
3. **Interest-based CTA** — validate interest with a small commitment before asking for the meeting.

## Step 3 — The "Mr. Miyagi" method for objections

Most objections are reactions to interruption, not product concerns. Redirect momentum instead of fighting:
1. **Agree** with the objection to disarm defensiveness.
2. **Incentivize the conversation** with clarifying questions.
3. **Sell the test drive**, not the full commitment — keep them exploring, not defending.
