# Fact-Check: verifying claims from the collected material

The brief (and the job description) specifically reward verifying claims and catching weak reasoning. So instead of taking the experts' numbers at face value, I pulled out the specific, checkable claims and tried to confirm each one against an independent source.

The most useful thing I noticed while doing this: there's a real difference between claims you can independently verify and numbers only the author can see (reply rates, conversion %, "reps trained"). The second kind isn't necessarily false, but it's marketing until proven, so I'd treat it as directional rather than gospel.

## Verdict key
- ✅ Confirmed: an independent source agrees
- 🟡 Self-reported: plausible and repeated, but not independently auditable
- ❌ Wrong or misleading: contradicted by evidence

## What this tells me

A couple of things stood out once I'd gone through all of them:

The biographical and structural claims (acquisitions, book status, dataset sizes) all checked out. So these people genuinely are who they say they are, which is a good sign for source quality.

The performance numbers (reply rates, conversion %, calls made) are almost all self-reported. That's not dishonesty, it's just the nature of sales content. But any playbook built on them should treat them as hypotheses to A/B test in our own funnel, not as guarantees.

So the practical takeaway is simple: borrow the frameworks, but re-verify the numbers on our own data.
