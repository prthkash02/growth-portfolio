# Fact-Check — verifying claims from the collected material

The brief (and the job description) explicitly reward verifying claims and catching weak reasoning. So rather than take the experts' numbers at face value, I pulled the specific, checkable claims out of the collected content and tried to confirm each against an **independent** source.

The most useful distinction I found: separate **independently verifiable facts** from **practitioner self-reports** (numbers only the author can see — reply rates, conversion %, "reps trained"). The second kind isn't necessarily false, but it's marketing until proven, and a good operator treats it as *directional*, not gospel.

## Verdict key
- ✅ **Confirmed** — independent source agrees
- 🟡 **Self-reported** — plausible, repeated, but not independently auditable
- ❌ **Wrong / misleading** — contradicted by evidence

## What this tells me (the judgment layer)
- The **structural/biographical** claims (acquisitions, book status, dataset sizes) all check out — these people are who they say they are. Good sign for source quality.
- The **performance numbers** (reply rates, conversion %, calls made) are almost all self-reported. That's not dishonesty — it's the nature of sales content — but a playbook built on them should frame them as *hypotheses to A/B test in our own funnel*, not guarantees.
- Practical takeaway for the eventual playbook: **borrow the frameworks, re-verify the numbers on our own data.**
