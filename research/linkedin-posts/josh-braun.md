## 1. The 4-part framework for a clear message (Problem · Premise · Payoff · Prompt)
**Posted:** ~late 2025 · [permalink](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josh-braun_if-youre-doing-outbound-your-job-is-simple-share-7406295073413345280-BmAT)

If you're doing outbound, your job is simple: **pique curiosity.** When people are curious, they talk, and more conversations with the right people = more money. In a sea of noise, clarity is your advantage, but most people were never taught to explain things clearly. Clarity isn't a personality trait, it's a learnable skill. Four parts, all starting with P:

- **Problem**: "SDRs usually have 1-3 conversations per 50 dials because most people don't pick up, even with direct dials."
- **Premise**: "With X, you give us a list of people you want to talk to, and your reps know who's most likely to answer the phone."
- **Payoff**: "So instead of 1-3 conversations per 50 dials, they're having 10-13, and they stop wasting time calling people who were never going to pick up."
- **Prompt**: "What are your thoughts on that?"

No hype, no forcing, just a clear explanation that invites a response. That's how curiosity starts.

## 2. The problem isn't the number of touches: it's the intent
**Posted:** ~2025 · [permalink](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josh-braun_this-is-what-a-typical-sales-cadence-looks-share-7323034155099828226-xfyH)

A typical sales cadence: 8+ touches in 7 days, continuing for 28 days, email, call, LinkedIn, repeat. No wonder buyers think salespeople are annoying; we've trained them to brace for the pitch and tune us out. But the problem isn't the touch count, it's the **intent** behind them. Are you reaching out to explore fit, or to get the meeting at all costs? The right message with the wrong energy still feels like a shove.

Try instead:
- Send **3-4 emails over a few weeks**, each highlighting a single relevant problem.
- No response? That's a response.
- Interview a customer who recently switched; let them describe what wasn't working, **they'll write your messaging for you.**

> "Good outbound copy isn't written, it's found. Lean back."
